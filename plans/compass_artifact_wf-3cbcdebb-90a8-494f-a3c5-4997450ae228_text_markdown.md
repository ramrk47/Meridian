# Study Planning & Progress Tracking for Indian Medical PG Aspirants: Market Research for a Cross-Platform Adherence Planner

## TL;DR
- **The cross-platform orchestration bet (Hypothesis #1) is directionally validated but under-proven**: aspirants demonstrably combine Marrow + PrepLadder + own notes and hand-build schedules, but a clean "I want ONE tool to orchestrate my platforms" quote remains INFERRED rather than directly OBSERVED. The strongest, most observable behavior is the **adherence/accountability gap** — aspirants relentlessly seek study buddies to set and check daily targets — which is the deepest moat for the product.
- **Time-logging is genuinely double-edged, not pure vanity**: YPT-style hour tracking visibly motivates a meaningful segment through gamification and real-time group ranking, while others find it anxiety-inducing and abandon rigid plans within 3–4 days. The defensible product position is adherence + coverage as the primary metric, with hours as an optional, de-emphasized signal — not the removal of hours entirely.
- **Backward-planning from a fixed exam date, multi-revision (M1/M2/M3) scheduling, and "am I behind?" anxiety are the clearest unmet needs** — every topper strategy and coaching framework is organized around revision passes and a countdown to the exam, yet existing tools plan forward, which is precisely why plans collapse at revision cycles and in the final-phase panic.

## Key Findings

1. **Adherence, not hours, is the real job-to-be-done — and it shows up as a demand for human accountability.** The single most recurrent, directly observed behavior is aspirants seeking "study buddies" / accountability partners to set daily targets and compare daily progress. This is observed repeatedly on NEET PG study-buddy forums.
2. **Cross-platform combining is real.** Aspirants explicitly list stacks like "Marrow Edition 8 + PrepLadder" plus their own notes. The orchestration *desire* is inferred from this behavior plus the proliferation of DIY spreadsheets/Notion dashboards, not from a verbatim feature request.
3. **Plans are abandoned fast.** The recurring failure mode: a timetable "may work for 3–4 days but in the long run it will ultimately fail," tied to "exceptional planning skills but poor executive skills." This is the central intention–completion gap the product targets.
4. **Revision and the final phase are where tools break.** Toppers and coaching frameworks universally organize prep around multiple revision passes and a fixed countdown; no consumer tool plans backward from the exam date.
5. **The market is large and concentrated.** Per NBEMS, 2,42,493 candidates registered for NEET PG 2025 and 2,30,114 appeared — conducted as India's largest single-shift computer-based exam across 1,052 centres in 301 cities. A large, anxious, time-pressed, exam-date-synchronized user base is exactly the condition under which a backward-planning adherence tool has leverage.

## Details

### Hypothesis 1 — Cross-platform rotation plans and desire to orchestrate (PRIMARY)
**Verdict: Directionally validated; combining behavior OBSERVED, orchestration desire INFERRED.**

- OBSERVED: Aspirants combine multiple paid platforms. A final-year NEET PG aspirant lists "Using: Marrow Edition 8 + PrepG [PrepLadder]"; INI-CET aspirants describe "self preparing from marrow alone" while others run PrepLadder as primary. Faculty-comparison spreadsheets that map educators across "Prepladder, Marrow, DocTutorials, and others" circulate on Scribd — evidence aspirants think in terms of mixing faculty across platforms.
- OBSERVED: A live Google Sheet titled "Marrow Study Planner" exists and is shared publicly, alongside Scribd documents like "NEET PG Study Plan Marrow." These are the displacement targets.
- OBSERVED: Marrow's own "12-week Preparation Strategy" blog and PrepLadder's "90-Day" timetable are heavily commented, with aspirants asking to compress/adjust them ("can't u finish in 30–35 days max") — showing aspirants actively re-edit canned plans to fit their reality.
- INFERRED (flagged): A direct "I wish one tool unified Marrow + PrepLadder + my notes" statement was NOT found. The desire is reasonable extrapolation from (a) combining behavior, (b) DIY spreadsheet/Notion building, and (c) the existence of AI-planner startups (Dream PG, OnCourse) targeting exactly this.

**Implication:** The bet is sound but the riskiest assumption. The product should treat orchestration as a hypothesis to validate quickly, while leaning on the *proven* adherence/accountability demand as the wedge.

### Hypothesis 2 — Time-logging as vanity vs. genuine need
**Verdict: Mixed — both sides real. Reframe, don't remove.**

- OBSERVED (pro-tracking): A NEET-SS aspirant wrote a first-person account of using YPT (Yeolpumta), setting a "minimum target of 6 hours," competing on logged hours with a NEET PG group, and explicitly calling it "Gamifying studying… so was good for me and helped me secure a decent rank." YPT (Yeolpumta), developed by Pallo Inc and marketed to "5 million people all over the world," foregrounds "Realtime ranking: Get to know your rank in the same study category." App reviews capture the competitive-hours culture verbatim: "I feel like it's funner to challenge myself to go longer than others, haha... some ppl go 13hrs..my brain would be burnt out. But! This is a good app!!!"
- OBSERVED (anti-tracking / adherence-first): A NEET PG Quora answer argues against rigid timetables outright — a plan "may work for 3–4 days but in the long run it will ultimately fail." Another aspirant: "exceptional planning skills but poor executive skills… I used to make a good schedule everytime I sat for revision [but] was not able to [follow it]." The same leaderboard mechanic that motivates some ("Compete with friends and global community on study time rankings") is what reviewers say "can get kinda stressful from all the comparison, making it easy to burn out."
- INFERRED: What aspirants consistently ask peers to check is *task completion* ("complete daily tasks," "compare our daily progress report"), not hours — supporting the thesis that adherence/coverage is the deeper need, with hours as a motivational layer for a subset.

**Implication:** Make adherence ("did I do what I planned?") and coverage ("did I cover high-yield?") the hero metrics; offer hours as an optional, auto-derived, non-competitive stat to retain the gamification segment without manufacturing the anxiety that drives abandonment.

### Hypothesis 3 — Breakdown at revision cycles and the "last 10 days" panic; no backward planning
**Verdict: Strongly supported (mostly via toppers/coaching frameworks; aspirant-panic quotes INFERRED).**

- OBSERVED: Backward, revision-centric planning is the universal mental model. A Quora answer on NEET PG active revision: "suppose I have 4 months left… 1.5 months for 2nd reading, 1 month for 3rd, 1 more month for 4th and last 12 days revision" — literal backward planning from the exam date into M1/M2/M3+ passes. Toppers stress "at least 2–3 revisions before the D-day."
- OBSERVED: The final-phase intensification is real and exam-anchored. NEET PG 2024 AIR 1 Dr Vaibhav Garg (MBBS from GMCH Chandigarh, a perfect 100 percentile scorer) reportedly "studied 4-5 hours a day and in [the] final three months before NEET PG, he studied up to 12 hours" — a named topper validating the countdown-driven ramp. The final-phase panic is also a named content genre — "How to prepare in the last 1 month," "FINAL REVISION STRATEGY," last-10-days mock cadence ("increasing to 2 per week in last 10 days"). Coaching content repeatedly warns "Don't put off realizing you're behind for three months."
- OBSERVED: Every framework is structured as three phases (foundation → revision+mocks → rapid revision) anchored to the exam date, yet consumer planning tools (YPT, Notion templates) are forward-logging, not exam-anchored.
- INFERRED: Direct "am I behind?" panic posts from r/indianmedschool were not retrievable, but the overwhelming volume of last-month/revision strategy content is strong indirect evidence of the demand.

**Implication:** Backward planning from a locked exam date, with auto-scheduled revision passes (M1/M2/M3) and a live "are you on track?" indicator, is the single most differentiated feature and directly attacks the failure point.

### Hypothesis 4 — Editable, shareable plans; copying toppers; accountability pods
**Verdict: Strongly validated (OBSERVED).**

- OBSERVED (sharing/accountability — strongest evidence in the whole study): Dozens of NEET PG / INI-CET study-buddy posts seeking accountability: "looking for a study buddy to help set and achieve daily goals together"; "check and compare our daily progress report for further accountability"; "set targets, discuss topics"; "make daily targets and check upon the progress"; "study together on video call with audio off and keep a check on each other." Female-only study-group requests drew high engagement (28 replies on one thread).
- OBSERVED (copying toppers): Topper strategy interviews (NEET PG '24 AIR 1 Dr. Vaibhav Garg; PrepLadder/Marrow topper success-mantra series) are a major content genre; aspirants seek "toppers' timetables." Canned topper/faculty plans (Marrow, PrepLadder) are downloaded and re-edited.
- OBSERVED (editable): Aspirants comment on canned plans asking to compress or rebalance subjects — they treat plans as forkable drafts, not fixed prescriptions.

**Implication:** Editable + shareable plans and lightweight accountability pods are validated, low-risk features that map directly to existing behavior. The "study-with-me, mic-off, check on each other" pattern suggests a shared-progress view (not video) is enough.

### The Artifacts (what aspirants actually build — the displacement target)
- **Google Sheets ("Marrow Study Planner"):** Publicly shared spreadsheet planners exist. Structure (inferred from genre + visible templates): subject rows, video/QBank/notes columns, date columns, and revision-pass checkboxes; weekly or monthly grid views.
- **Scribd PDFs:** "NEET PG Study Plan Marrow," and faculty-comparison spreadsheets across Marrow/PrepLadder/DocTutorials. These reveal aspirants think in **faculty-by-subject rotation** across platforms (e.g., pick the best Pathology faculty on one app, best Pharm on another).
- **Notion dashboards:** A large market of NEET/JEE Notion templates exists (trackers, "revision list," "performance analysis," mock-test logs, doubt management, weekly study plans) — NEET PG-specific ones are fewer but the NEET-UG/JEE templates reveal the target mental model: subject-wise progress %, revision list, mock-test analytics, daily planner.
- **Mental model revealed:** Aspirants conceptualize prep as a **matrix of [subject × resource/faculty × revision pass]** counting down to a fixed exam date — exactly the structure a backward-planning orchestrator should encode. The recurring complaint across analogous markets: elaborate DIY dashboards "get abandoned by week three" because the user must build the plan from scratch without knowing the right pacing.

### Bonus Decisions — Signal
- **Scope to subscribed platforms only vs. all:** No direct aspirant statement found. Coaching advice strongly discourages multi-app sprawl ("Don't subscribe to multiple platforms," "stick to one QBank"), implying a planner scoped to the 1–2 platforms a user actually owns will feel more credible than an all-platform catalog. INFERRED.
- **Subscription expiring before exam:** No direct evidence found (notable gap). Marrow's no-refund policy and version-switching dynamics (Edition 8; PrepLadder "Version XI" with retained progress/analytics) suggest timing subscriptions to the exam is a real concern worth a discovery interview. UNCONFIRMED.
- **Daily vs weekly cadence:** Observed behavior favors **daily** targets and daily progress checks (study-buddy posts overwhelmingly say "daily goals/targets/progress"). Coaching templates provide both daily and weekly grids. Lean daily for execution, weekly for planning/review.
- **Why planners are abandoned after ~week 2:** Best evidence — rigid plans "work for 3–4 days" then fail; "poor executive skills"; analogous-market observation that DIY dashboards die "by week three" because users build pacing from scratch. Root causes: no recovery mechanism when a day is missed, no backward re-planning, and manual upkeep burden.

### What existing tools get wrong
- **YPT / hour-loggers:** measure input (time), not completion or coverage; real-time group ranking breeds comparison anxiety and burnout; no exam-date anchoring; no resource-level plan.
- **Notion/Sheets DIY:** infinitely flexible but high build/maintenance cost; no auto-rescheduling; abandoned when life disrupts the plan; no cross-platform "done" verification.
- **In-app planners (Marrow/PrepLadder/Dream PG/OnCourse):** locked to one platform; can't orchestrate a Marrow-QBank + PrepLadder-RR + own-notes rotation; "done" is siloed per app.
- **Canned topper/coaching plans:** static PDFs; not personalized to start date, subscription, or progress; no adherence feedback loop.

## Recommendations
1. **Lead with the proven wedge (accountability + adherence), not orchestration.** Ship editable, shareable plans + lightweight accountability pods (shared daily-target completion, mic-off "study-with-me" status) first — this maps to the most strongly observed behavior and carries the least adoption risk. Benchmark: if >40% of weekly-active users join or form a pod within 2 weeks, double down; if not, treat the social layer as optional.
2. **Make backward planning from a fixed exam date the signature feature.** Auto-generate M1/M2/M3 revision passes and a live "on track / X days behind" indicator that **re-plans automatically when a day is missed** (the missing recovery mechanism). This directly attacks week-2 abandonment and last-phase panic.
3. **Reframe tracking around coverage and adherence; keep hours optional.** Auto-derive a "done-diary" from real activity across platforms; show coverage of high-yield topics and adherence %. Offer hours as a quiet, non-ranked stat to retain the YPT-motivated segment without importing its anxiety.
4. **Validate orchestration with a narrow MVP scoped to subscribed platforms.** Start with Marrow + PrepLadder + "own notes," let users build a [subject × faculty × revision-pass] rotation, and test whether they value unified "done" verification. Threshold to expand to more platforms: a clear majority of users manually adding a second platform's tasks.
5. **Run targeted discovery on the two evidence gaps:** (a) the explicit "I want one tool to orchestrate my apps" demand, and (b) subscription-expiry-before-exam behavior. These are the assumptions most likely to break the model.
6. **Design against the abandonment causes:** zero-friction daily check-in, automatic catch-up rescheduling, and a forkable "copy a topper's plan" onboarding so users never start from a blank page.

## Caveats
- **Reddit gap:** Despite r/indianmedschool being the priority source, no posts were directly retrievable through available tools; frequency claims about that community are INFERRED. Much accountability evidence comes from an analogous NEET PG study-buddy forum (MoocLab), which is directly comparable but not r/indianmedschool.
- **Directional, not quantified:** Reddit/forum vote and comment counts were largely unfetchable; recurrence is treated as directional signal by thread-genre, not measured frequency.
- **Some sources are coaching/SEO content** (PrepLadder, Marrow, DocTutorials blogs). These reliably reveal the *mental model* (phases, revision passes, last-month panic) but are marketing, not neutral aspirant voice; weighted accordingly.
- **Two material gaps remain unconfirmed:** the explicit orchestration desire (H1) and subscription-expiry behavior — both flagged for primary discovery before heavy build investment.
- **Forward-looking items** (AI planner startups Dream PG, OnCourse) indicate competitive interest in this space but their traction is unverified. One named claim encountered in research (an "INI-CET/NEET PG AIR 11 Dr. Nishid") could not be independently verified and is excluded from findings.
